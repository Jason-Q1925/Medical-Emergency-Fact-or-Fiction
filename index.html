House S3:E14 — Tapeworm Removal Surgery

Hannah contracted a fish tapeworm, also known as Diphyllobothrium latum, from eating raw/undercooked infected trout/perch from a lake. The parasite established itself in her small intestine after she ate the contaminated fish and, over time, it grew to 25 feet. The small intestine is about 20 feet, so it would have to wrap around itself a bit.

In the show, the fish tapeworm was absorbing Hannah's Vitamin B12. As it fed, it absorbed nearly all of the vitamin B12 from Hannah's digested food, driving her into severe megaloblastic anemia—a hematic disorder where the bone marrow produces abnormally large and structurally immature red blood cells. Due to her megaloblastic anemia, her brain eventually got affected from the lack of B12, causing her seizures.

when Hannah had her surgery, she didn't have anesthesia because she has CIPA so she can't feel pain anyway, meaning she was seeing the whole process unfold right in front of her eyes. Most people would be scared, but I wouldn't because I'm so tuff 🥶🥶

What House Did vs. What Would Actually Happen

In the show, House simply physically opens up an incision in her small intestine, reaches into her gut with his hands and a pair of forceps, and manually pulls the wriggling, 25-foot-long live tapeworm directly out of her body.

If the procedure was done in the real world, the tapeworm would break since they are extremely fragile. Pulling it manually would likely snap the body, leaving the head (scolex) securely hooked into the intestinal wall, allowing the entire worm to just grow right back. Additionally, Cutting open a patient's bowel during surgery exposes the sterile abdominal cavity to highly dangerous gut bacteria. In the real world, this would likely cause peritonitis—a disease where the thin inner lining of the abdominal tissue experiences inflammation—and fatal septic shock, which is when infectious entities within body causing things like organ failure, blood vessel leakage, and low blood pressure.

What would happen in the real world if left untreated

Prolonged B12 deficiency from an untreated fish tapeworm triggers a condition called subacute combined degeneration of the spinal cord. In this condition, the myelin sheaths protecting nerve fibers begin to break down. Since neural cells don't go through mitosis and regenerate, past a certain threshold, the breakdown of myelin sheaths cannot be reversed or "saved."

While a tapeworm taking vitamin B12 in the small intestine may not seem like it could problems in the brain, it only indirectly causes problems. Without enough B12, homocysteine and methylmalonic acid can build up from within the body, which causes the brain to become overstimulated and cause seizures. As this continues, it can cause permanent nerve damage. Another thing that a lack of B12 causes is megaloblastic anemia, since B12 is a factor influencing the structure of red blood cells. Both the nerve damage and the megaloblastic anemia can contribute to extreme moods and organ failure, eventually leading to death. During this process the "window" for full recovery is still there, but it would need to be acted upon as soon as possible.

Fact or Fiction?

Fiction
While the actual tapeworm and effects are real, the procedure for its removal is fiction. While a Diphyllobothrium latum tapeworm can grow to 25 feet and cause vitamin B12-deficiency, leading to megaloblastic anemia and seizures, no doctor would or should hand-extract one when some anthelmintic pills are more clean and effective.

Biology Spotlight

Anatomy of Diphyllobothrium latum

The scolex grips the intestinal wall with two elongated, groove-like suckers called bothria. Below it, the neck is the true danger zone — the region of cell division that generates the entire chain of body segments (proglottids). Gravid proglottids at the tail are packed with fertilized eggs and are shed into the host's stool.

Real Medications

How Doctors Actually Kill Tapeworms

Praziquantel (Biltricide®) — First choice
Causes rapid, sustained muscle contraction and spasm, paralysing the scolex. The worm detaches and is excreted. Also damages the worm's outer membrane.
Albendazole (Albenza®)
Inhibits microtubule synthesis inside the worm's cells, blocking glucose uptake. Used when praziquantel is unavailable or for systemic larval infections.
Nitazoxanide (Alinia®)
Disrupts the worm's anaerobic energy metabolism. Broad-spectrum; often used in younger patients.
Diagnostic Methods

How to Confirm Infection

  • Ova and parasite (O&P) stool examination — checks for eggs and segments
  • Species identification under microscope determines dose
  • Serum B12 level — confirms deficiency severity
  • Full blood count for megaloblastic anemia markers
  • MRI if neurological symptoms suggest spinal cord involvement

The Emergency

When Blood Pressure Collapses

A severe traumatic head injury ruptures major blood vessels, triggering rapid, massive hemorrhage. As blood volume plummets, blood pressure collapses — and without adequate pressure, even healthy red blood cells can't circulate fast enough to deliver oxygen to critical organs.

The anime depicts this as a cascading environmental disaster inside the body: temperatures plunge (hypothermia), workers collapse from exhaustion (oxygen-starved tissues), and infrastructure breaks down (failing organs). It's dramatic, but it's also a surprisingly faithful model of what hypovolemic shock actually feels like from the body's perspective.

If Left Untreated

The Lethal Triad of Trauma

Hemorrhagic shock untreated doesn't just cause one problem — it causes three that amplify each other in a vicious spiral that trauma surgeons call the "lethal triad." Each element worsens the other two.

🥶
Hypothermia
⚗️
Acidosis
🩸
Coagulopathy

Hypothermia impairs clotting enzyme function. Acidosis (lactic acid buildup from oxygen-starved tissues) further disables those same enzymes. Coagulopathy (the blood losing its ability to clot) makes the bleeding worse — which deepens the acidosis — which worsens the hypothermia. Past a certain point, the cycle cannot be interrupted without massive surgical intervention.

⚡ Did You Know? ▾
"Why do trauma surgeons deliberately keep some patients slightly hypothermic during surgery?"
In certain types of cardiac and vascular surgery — particularly those requiring circulatory arrest — surgeons use controlled therapeutic hypothermia to slow cellular metabolism and buy extra time before brain damage sets in. Cooling the brain to around 18°C can extend the safe window of circulatory arrest from under 4 minutes to up to 40–60 minutes. The same physiology that kills in trauma is weaponized as a tool in the operating room.
Real-World Treatment Protocol

What Happens in the Trauma Bay

Hemorrhagic shock treatment follows a strict order of operations designed to interrupt the lethal triad before it becomes irreversible:

  • Stop the bleeding — direct pressure, tourniquets, or emergency surgical ligation
  • Fluid resuscitation — IV crystalloids (saline) temporarily prop up blood pressure while blood is typed
  • Type and cross-match — compatibility testing to prevent fatal immune reaction to transfused blood
  • Packed RBCs + plasma transfusion — the definitive treatment, administered in ~1:1:1 ratio with platelets
  • Warming — heated blankets and warmed IV fluids actively fight hypothermia throughout
  • Monitoring — continuous lactate measurement confirms tissues are receiving adequate oxygen

Biology Spotlight

Hemorrhagic Shock — Class System

Doctors classify hemorrhagic shock on a four-tier scale based on estimated blood loss and physiological response:

  • Class I — up to 750 mL lost; no change in vital signs; body self-compensates
  • Class II — 750–1,500 mL; heart rate rises; body straining to compensate
  • Class III — 1,500–2,000 mL; BP drops; altered mental status; needs transfusion
  • Class IV — over 2,000 mL; life-threatening; immediate surgery and transfusion
What a Transfusion Provides

Packed RBCs vs. Whole Blood

Modern trauma centres typically use "component therapy" — separate bags of packed red blood cells, plasma (clotting factors), and platelets — rather than whole blood, allowing precise targeting of specific deficits.

However, there's growing evidence that whole blood transfusion (used widely in military trauma) may be superior in severe hemorrhage because it replaces everything simultaneously, reducing the time-to-coagulation-factor-replacement.

Cells at Work! Accuracy Notes

What the Anime Got Right

  • Red blood cells cannot function below a threshold oxygen partial pressure — depicted accurately
  • Platelets as small, round, child-like characters — accurate to their size relative to RBCs
  • The "blizzard" metaphor for hypothermia — physiologically apt
  • Reinforcement cells arriving via transfusion as a unified force — accurate depiction of volume restoration
  • Heart working visibly harder — reflects compensatory tachycardia during shock

The underlying biology is nearly textbook-accurate. The anime correctly depicts the pressure drop, the hypothermia, the exhausted red blood cells, and the necessity of transfusion. It earns its accuracy badge.

The Emergency

A Rusted Bar Through the Gut

During an ambush at the University of Eastern Colorado, Joel is tackled from a second-story balcony and lands directly on a piece of exposed, vertical rebar. The bar penetrates through his torso, creating both an entry and exit wound through the abdominal cavity.

The abdominal cavity contains densely packed, highly vascular structures: the abdominal aorta, inferior vena cava, mesenteric arteries, liver, intestines, and kidneys. A bar impaling through that region at that velocity would almost certainly strike at least one of them.

Without Treatment

The Real Countdown After Impalement

Minutes 1–5
If a major vessel is severed (aorta, vena cava, mesenteric artery): exsanguination and death. The rebar, while in place, may partially tamponade the bleeding — removal collapses that effect instantly.
Hours 1–6
If somehow still alive: bowel bacteria flood the sterile peritoneal cavity. Peritonitis sets in — the abdominal lining becomes violently inflamed. Fever spikes. Pain becomes extraordinary.
Days 1–3
Peritonitis progresses to sepsis — bacteria and their toxins enter the bloodstream. The immune response begins destroying the patient's own organs. Blood pressure crashes.
Days 3–7
Septic shock → multi-organ failure. Without IV antibiotics, surgical washout, and ICU-level support, the mortality rate approaches 100%.
⚡ Did You Know? ▾
"Is penicillin actually the right antibiotic for a bowel perforation — even if it had been given immediately?"
No — and this is a critical distinction. Penicillin is narrow-spectrum: it's excellent against gram-positive organisms like Streptococcus, but bowel perforation releases a massive, mixed flora of gram-negative bacteria (like E. coli and Klebsiella) and anaerobes (like Bacteroides fragilis) that penicillin cannot cover. Real protocol calls for broad-spectrum IV combinations like piperacillin-tazobactam, or a carbapenems in severe cases. Even if Ellie had given the penicillin on day one, it would not have covered the primary pathogens involved.
Real Emergency Protocol

How Trauma Surgeons Handle Impalement

Impalement injuries have a strict field and surgical protocol that is almost the exact opposite of what Ellie does in the game:

  • Do NOT remove the object on scene — it may be the only thing preventing exsanguination
  • Stabilize the object in place using bulky dressings and, if needed, cut surrounding material
  • Transport urgently to a trauma center with the object still in situ
  • Exploratory laparotomy in the OR — open the abdomen, visualize all damage
  • Controlled removal with surgical clamps immediately controlling any bleeding vessels
  • Repair of torn organs — bowel resection and reanastomosis if perforation present
  • Thorough abdominal washout (irrigation) to remove contamination
  • Broad-spectrum IV antibiotics started pre-operatively and continued post-op
"Why do emergency protocols explicitly forbid removing an impaled object at the scene, even if the patient is in pain?"
Biology Spotlight

What's in the Abdominal Cavity

The abdomen is one of the most densely vascular regions of the body. A rebar impaling through it at velocity could strike any combination of:

  • Abdominal aorta — main arterial trunk; rupture is almost immediately fatal
  • Inferior vena cava — massive venous return; rupture causes rapid exsanguination
  • Liver — highly vascular; even partial lacerations cause significant hemorrhage
  • Bowel — perforation releases billions of bacteria directly into the peritoneum
  • Mesenteric vessels — supply the intestines; damage causes bowel ischemia
  • Kidneys — retroperitoneal; bleed heavily when lacerated
Why Penicillin Was Wrong

The Antibiotic Mismatch

Bowel flora is polymicrobial — a complex, mixed community of gram-positive, gram-negative, and anaerobic organisms. Penicillin covers only a narrow subset of these, missing the most dangerous gram-negative and anaerobic species entirely.

Standard empirical coverage for abdominal perforation typically combines a beta-lactam (like piperacillin) with a beta-lactamase inhibitor (tazobactam), plus metronidazole for anaerobic coverage — none of which are penicillin alone.

What Peritonitis Feels Like

The Clinical Signature

Peritonitis — infection of the peritoneal lining — presents with a classic, distinctive physical sign called "board-like rigidity." The abdominal wall becomes involuntarily rigid, as if made of wood, a reflex the body uses to guard the inflamed cavity from any movement. Touching the abdomen causes excruciating pain. Any movement — breathing, coughing — becomes agonizing. It's one of the clearest signs in all of emergency medicine.

  • Board-like abdominal rigidity — involuntary guarding
  • Rebound tenderness — pain worsens when pressure is released, not applied
  • High fever with rigors (shaking chills)
  • Absent bowel sounds — paralytic ileus
  • Rapid progression to septic shock without treatment
