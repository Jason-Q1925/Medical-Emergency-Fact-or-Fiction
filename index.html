House S3:E14 — Tapeworm Removal Surgery

Hannah contracted a fish tapeworm, also known as Diphyllobothrium latum, from eating raw/undercooked infected trout/perch from a lake. The parasite established itself in her small intestine after she ate the contaminated fish and, over time, it grew to 25 feet. The small intestine is about 20 feet, so it would have to wrap around itself a bit.

In the show, the fish tapeworm was absorbing Hannah's Vitamin B12. As it fed, it absorbed nearly all of the vitamin B12 from Hannah's digested food, driving her into severe megaloblastic anemia—a hematic disorder where the bone marrow produces abnormally large and structurally immature red blood cells. Due to her megaloblastic anemia, her brain eventually got affected from the lack of B12, causing her seizures.

when Hannah had her surgery, she didn't have anesthesia because she has CIPA so she can't feel pain anyway, meaning she was seeing the whole process unfold right in front of her eyes. Most people would be scared, but I wouldn't because I'm so tuff 🥶🥶

What House Did vs. What Would Actually Happen

In the show, House simply physically opens up an incision in her small intestine, reaches into her gut with his hands and a pair of forceps, and manually pulls the wriggling, 25-foot-long live tapeworm directly out of her body.

If the procedure was done in the real world, the tapeworm would break since they are extremely fragile. Pulling it manually would likely snap the body, leaving the head (scolex) securely hooked into the intestinal wall, allowing the entire worm to just grow right back. Additionally, Cutting open a patient's bowel during surgery exposes the sterile abdominal cavity to highly dangerous gut bacteria. In the real world, this would likely cause peritonitis—a disease where the thin inner lining of the abdominal tissue experiences inflammation—and fatal septic shock, which is when infectious entities within body causing things like organ failure, blood vessel leakage, and low blood pressure.

What would happen in the real world if left untreated

Prolonged B12 deficiency from an untreated fish tapeworm triggers a condition called subacute combined degeneration of the spinal cord. In this condition, the myelin sheaths protecting nerve fibers begin to break down. Since neural cells don't go through mitosis and regenerate, past a certain threshold, the breakdown of myelin sheaths cannot be reversed or "saved."

While a tapeworm taking vitamin B12 in the small intestine may not seem like it could problems in the brain, it only indirectly causes problems. Without enough B12, homocysteine and methylmalonic acid can build up from within the body, which causes the brain to become overstimulated and cause seizures. As this continues, it can cause permanent nerve damage. Another thing that a lack of B12 causes is megaloblastic anemia, since B12 is a factor influencing the structure of red blood cells. Both the nerve damage and the megaloblastic anemia can contribute to extreme moods and organ failure, eventually leading to death. During this process the "window" for full recovery is still there, but it would need to be acted upon as soon as possible.

Fact or Fiction?

Fiction
While the actual tapeworm and effects are real, the procedure for its removal is fiction. While a Diphyllobothrium latum tapeworm can grow to 25 feet and cause vitamin B12-deficiency, leading to megaloblastic anemia and seizures, no doctor would or should hand-extract one when some anthelmintic pills are more clean and effective.

Biology Spotlight

Anatomy of Diphyllobothrium latum

The scolex grips the intestinal wall with two elongated, groove-like suckers called bothria. Below it, the neck is the true danger zone — the region of cell division that generates the entire chain of body segments (proglottids). Gravid proglottids at the tail are packed with fertilized eggs and are shed into the host's stool.

Real Medications

How Doctors Actually Kill Tapeworms

Praziquantel (Biltricide®) — First choice
Causes rapid, sustained muscle contraction and spasm, paralysing the scolex. The worm detaches and is excreted. Also damages the worm's outer membrane.
Albendazole (Albenza®)
Inhibits microtubule synthesis inside the worm's cells, blocking glucose uptake. Used when praziquantel is unavailable or for systemic larval infections.
Nitazoxanide (Alinia®)
Disrupts the worm's anaerobic energy metabolism. Broad-spectrum; often used in younger patients.
Diagnostic Methods

How to Confirm Infection

  • Ova and parasite (O&P) stool examination — checks for eggs and segments
  • Species identification under microscope determines dose
  • Serum B12 level — confirms deficiency severity
  • Full blood count for megaloblastic anemia markers
  • MRI if neurological symptoms suggest spinal cord involvement

Cells at Work! S1:E12–13 — Hemorrhagic shock sequence

The Emergency

In the show, the human body experiences severe traumatic head injury and ruptures major blood vessels, triggering rapid, massive hemorrhage. Due to the blood volume decreasing, the blood pressure also plummets proportioanlly as a resul. Without adequate pressure, red blood cells can't circulate fast enough to deliver oxygen to critical organs.

The anime depicts this event as an environmental disaster inside the body where temperatures plunge (due to hypothermia), workers collapse from exhaustion (since they lack enough oxygen), and infrastructure breaks down (representing failing organs).

How it was depicted v.s. what it actually means

When the body experiences massive earthquakes, structural collapses, and a freezing blizzard, it signifies hypovolemic shock — an emergency where severe blood or fluid loss (usually more than 20% of the body's total volume) prevents the heart from pumping enough blood to the body — and the "lethal triad" of trauma (this will be explained in the "if the emergency is left untreated" section). The earthquakes represent a plunge in blood pressure caused by rapid blood loss, while the freezing blizzard illustrates clinical hypothermia, which occurs because the massive drop in red blood cells makes the body lack its ability to distribute heat, leading to a loss of temperature regulation. For the treatment, a massive mechanical hatch opens in the sky to release millions of new red blood cells wearing slightly different uniforms and speaking a different dialect, which is depicting an intravenous (within/into the veins) blood transfusion. The slight variations in the new cells symbolize that the donor blood originated from a completely different human body, reinforcing the concept of blood compatiblity.

How blood transfusions work in the real world

When a patient this type of trauma in the real world, the traditional treatment is component therapy, where the separate components of blood are "gathered" to give the patient a blood transfusion they are compatible with to survive. Packed red blood cells are created by spinning whole blood in a device called a centrifuge to remove the liquid plasma, leaving a highly concentrated collection of RBCs. Transfusing these cells rapidly boosts the body's ability to deliver oxygen via hemoglobin (a set of 4 proteins that bind oxygen to iron) to vital organs like the brain and heart without overloading them with excess fluid. Additionally, in a blood transfusion, a plasma transfusion can also happen, which is the delivery of the straw-colored liquid portion of the blood, consisting of water, ions, nutrients, wastes, gases, and proteins called clotting factors. The primary purpose of plasma is hemostasis, which is the clotting of blood vessels to stop bleeding. Hemostasis is done by platelets flowing through the blood and aggregating to form a "web" of platelets that temporarily stop the bleeding, then the cells of the blood vessel tissue go through mitosis to heal the injury.

The Lethal Triad of Trauma

If the hemorrhagic shock is left untreated, it causes 3 problems known as the lethal triad of trauma, where each element can worsen the other two, which worsens the body's "situation." The lethal triad consists of the following 3:

Hypothermia: Hypothermia is a condition where the body's temperature is below what it should be, hence the word roots hypo (meaning below) and thermia (meaning temperature). Often, it is caused by the body being in environments that are extremely cold, but hemorrhages can also cause hypothermia since the body's circulation isn't able to circulate heat and oxygen sufficently. Hypothermia can impair the clotting factor function because the cold temperature slows down the particles of the clotting factors, making them lack the amount of energy they need to begin the clotting.

Acidosis: Acidosis is a state where the body's fluids, mainly the blood, become abnormally acidic, meaning the pH level drops below the normal baseline of 7.35 to 7.45. In the context of severe trauma and blood loss, this is typically called metabolic lactic acidosis. When a patient loses a massive amount of blood, their cells are don't have enough of the oxygen usually delivered by red blood cells. To stay alive, the cells are forced to switch from their normal, oxygen-reliant energy production to an emergency backup system called anaerobic metabolism. While anaerobic metabolism provides short-term energy, it can be dangerous in the long-term since the abnormal acid environment can cause organs impairment/failure and protein structure/function change. This is fatal if it's not treated properly with a blood transfusion.

Coagulopathy: Coagulopathy is a serious medical condition where the blood’s ability to form clots is impaired, leading to prolonged or excessive bleeding. Generally, this is caused by blood loss or one (or both) of the other lethal triad components. If all 3 lethal triad members are in play, the body's condition is worsened since they all "work together." Starting with hypothermia, the drop in body temperature impairs the clotting factors, while the acidic blood environment chemically alters and degrades these proteins. Because the coagulopathy is increased, it worsens the hypothermia and deepens the acidosis, causing the cycle again. Past a certain point, the cycle must be interrupted with massive surgical intervention or death is risked.

⚡ Did You Know? ▾
Trauma surgeons deliberately keep some patients slightly hypothermic during surgery
This is because in certain types of cardiac and vascular surgery surgeons use controlled therapeutic hypothermia to slow cellular metabolism and buy extra time before brain damage sets in. Cooling the brain to around 18°C can extend the safe window of circulatory arrest from under 4 minutes to up to 40–60 minutes.

Treatment and the order

Hemorrhagic shock treatment follows a strict order of operations designed to interrupt the lethal triad before it becomes irreversible:

  • Stop the bleeding: direct pressure, tourniquets, or emergency surgical ligation
  • Fluid resuscitation: IV (intravenous) crystalloids (saline) temporarily prop up blood pressure while blood is typed
  • Type and cross-match: compatibility testing to prevent fatal immune reaction to transfused blood
  • Packed RBCs + plasma transfusion: the definitive treatment, administered in ~1:1:1 ratio with platelets
  • Warming: heated blankets and warmed IV fluids actively fight hypothermia throughout
  • Monitoring: continuous lactate measurement confirms tissues are receiving adequate oxygen
Biology Spotlight

Hemorrhagic Shock — Class System

Doctors classify hemorrhagic shock on a four-tier scale based on estimated blood loss and physiological response:

  • Class I — up to 750 mL lost; no change in vital signs; body self-compensates
  • Class II — 750–1,500 mL; heart rate rises; body straining to compensate
  • Class III — 1,500–2,000 mL; BP drops; altered mental status; needs transfusion
  • Class IV — over 2,000 mL; life-threatening; immediate surgery and transfusion
What a Transfusion Provides

Packed RBCs vs. Whole Blood

Modern trauma centres typically use "component therapy" — separate bags of packed red blood cells, plasma (clotting factors), and platelets — rather than whole blood, allowing precise targeting of specific deficits.

However, there's growing evidence that whole blood transfusion (used widely in military trauma) may be superior in severe hemorrhage because it replaces everything simultaneously, reducing the time-to-coagulation-factor-replacement.

The Last of Us — Joel's rebar impalement and Ellie's field treatment

The Emergency

During an ambush at the University of Eastern Colorado, Joel is tackled from the second story of a building and lands directly on a piece of exposed rebar within the building. The bar penetrates through his torso, creating both an entry and exit wound through the abdomen.

The abdominal cavity contains important organs, which consist of the abdominal aorta, inferior vena cava, mesenteric arteries, liver, intestines, and kidneys. A bar impaling through that region at that height would almost certainly strike at least one of them.

Ellie's First "Aid" vs Real World Treatment

In the real world with a case like this, the first and most important rule is to not remove the impaled object on scene since it acts as a makeshift plug preventing immediate exsanguination (bleeding to death). Emergency responders would ensure the rusted metal rebar stays in place, cut the surrounding pieces of the rebar, then rush him to the hospital where a surgeon would perform an emergency laparotomy (surgery on the abdominal cavity). Then, the surgeons would extract the metal bar while simultaneously clamping and repairing shredded blood vessels & organs and extensively washing out the abdominal cavity to remove dirt and bacteria. Septic shock would be combatted with antibiotics and blood transfusions would be used to replenish lost blood pressure and volume.

Real Emergency Protocol

How Trauma Surgeons Handle Impalement

Impalement injuries have a strict field and surgical protocol that is almost the exact opposite of what Ellie does in the game:

  • Do NOT remove the object on scene — it may be the only thing preventing exsanguination
  • Stabilize the object in place using bulky dressings and, if needed, cut surrounding material
  • Transport urgently to a trauma center with the object still in situ
  • Exploratory laparotomy in the OR — open the abdomen, visualize all damage
  • Controlled removal with surgical clamps immediately controlling any bleeding vessels
  • Repair of torn organs — bowel resection and reanastomosis if perforation present
  • Thorough abdominal washout (irrigation) to remove contamination
  • Broad-spectrum IV antibiotics started pre-operatively and continued post-op

What's in the Abdominal Cavity

The abdomen is one of the most densely vascular regions of the body. Since it's so densely packed with vascular organs, a rebar impaling through it could strike any combination of the following organs:

  • Abdominal aorta: main arterial trunk
  • Inferior vena cava: massive venous return
  • Liver: filters toxins by producing bile and regulating blood cholesterol
  • Bowel: site of digestive processes
  • Mesenteric vessels: supply the intestines
  • Kidneys: filter blood and removes waste
